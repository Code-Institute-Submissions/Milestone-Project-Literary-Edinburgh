Classic literature

The Private Memoirs
and Confessions of
A Justified Sinner

James Hogg, 1824

Part psychological thriller, part mystery, part supernatural philosopy, part political and religous satire, James Hogg's masterpiece defies genre and was too far ahead of its time to be appreciated in the author's time. In the 20th century, however, Hogg's work - the apex of those two grand Scottish literary themes: the devil and the divided self - has become the subject of renwed study and attention the world over. An essential read for anyone who wants to understand the Scottish soul.

Buy now!

The Heart of Midlothian

Walter Scott, 1818

When her sister is unjustly imprisoned for infanticide the the Heart of Midlothian, the Old Tolbooth prison, Jeanie Deans travels from Edinburgh to London on foot. Her goal? To seek an audience with the square and to beg for a pardon that will save her sister. This novel didn't just cement Scott's reputation as the most beloved novelist of his age; it also succeeded in changing social attitudes and laws surrounding pregnancy and childbirth.

Buy now!

Catriona

Robert Louis Stevenson, 1893

Known and loved the world over for his novels such as Treasure Island and The Curious Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde, Robert Louis Stevenson created an epic adventure-love strory in Catriona. Follow David Balfour, hero of Stevenson's Kidnapped, as he battles to save the life of his friend, achieve freedom from prison, and share his life with his true love, Catriona.

Literary
fiction

Born Free

Laura Hird, 2000

From the same legendary Rebel Inc. writing stable as Irvine Welsh, Laura Hird takes us on a journey into the heart of contemporary working class Edinburgh. We view life through the eyes of four family members, parents Angie and Vic and teenagers Jake and Joanie. Hird’s witty and sharp writings sings throughout her debut novel.

Buy now!

The Prime of Miss Jean Brodie

Muriel Spark, 1961

Muriel Spark’s renowned novel about a young teacher with some unconventional ideas and methods won her worldwide acclaim. The novel was later turned into a film and has stood the test of time as a modern classic.

Buy now!

Filth

Irvine Welsh, 1998

Recently turned into a well-received film starring James McAvoy, Filth is probably Irvine Welsh’s most accessible novel for the non-Scot. This story of a corrupt Edinburgh policeman is written in a milder Scots dialect than many of Welsh’s other novels and upon publication instantly became a classic of transgressive fiction.

Buy now!

Historical fiction

The Fanatic

James Robertson, 2001

James Robertson’s debut novel takes place in both 1997 and 1670. When young outsider Andrew Carlin is asked to play the role of a religious fanatic who was tried and sentenced to death in the 17th century, he is drawn into a dark past that casts long shadows over the present.

Buy now!

The Observations

Jane Harris, 2007

In 1863, Bessy, a young woman trying to outrun her troubled past, begins work for Arabella, an eccentric and beautiful mistress, in a large house. But when Arabella gives her ever more bizarre and unusual jobs to do, Bessy soon realizes her new position may not be the saving grace it at first appeared. Shortlisted for numerous literary prizes, Jane Harris’s novel has won critical acclaim for it strong writing and even stronger heroine.

Buy now!

Joseph Knight

James Robertson, 2004

James Robertson’s acclaimed historical novel takes us from Enlightenment Edinburgh to the slave plantations of Jamaica. The novel is based on the true story of Joseph Knight, a one-time plantation slave who came to Scotland and won a landmark case that guaranteed not only his own freedom but the right of any human being to be free from slavery on Scottish soil.

Buy now!

Mysteries

Quite Ugly One Morning

Christopher Brookmyre, 1997

Christopher Brookmyre’s unique mix of crime, sharp humour and social commentary has proven to be a hit with UK readers for years. This dive into murder, social class and the inner workings of health system politics is sure to entertain.

One Good Turn

Kate Atkinson, 2007

When an act of road rage shocks the innocent bystanders waiting in line for a show at the Edinburgh festival, Jackson Brodie is unwittingly pulled into the events as a suspect. With each step he takes to prove his innocence, the case becomes ever more complicated. Kate Atkinson’s witty prose produces a tour de force and is bound to delight fans of the genre.

Science fiction

The Night Sessions

Ken MacLeod, 2009

Someone in futuristic Edinburgh is assassinating priests and bishops, and Detective Inspector Adam Ferguson is on the case. A sci-fi murder-mystery that takes on technology and religion in equal measure, The Night Sessions is a truly unique work from one of Scotland’s preeminent sci-fi authors.

The Bridge

Iain Banks, 2013

Set in a fictionalized version of Edinburgh’s stunning Forth Rail Bridge, Iain Banks takes us on an incredible journey into an entire futuristic society making its home within The Bridge. A truly remarkable novel and one that defies adequate description. Just read it!

Buy now!

Halting State

Charles Stross, 2008

When Sergeant Sue Smith investigates a break-in and robbery at a video games company she discovers that the perpetrators were the video game characters themselves, none other than a dragon and a squadron of orcs. But when real humans start dying the lines between games and the real world aren’t just blurred – they’re torn to shreds.

Romance

44 Scotland Street

Alexander McCall Smith, 2005

Alexander McCall Smith’s series of beloved vignettes began life as serialized tales in The Scotsman newspaper. In the tradition of Amistad Maupin’s Tales of the City, 44 Scotland Street grew to become a series of books that made its setting an integral character and delighted readers the world over.

In 1988, Emma and Dexter meet at their graduation from Edinburgh University. On July 15th. In David Nicholl’s worldwide bestseller, we follow the couple on that day every year in an epic love story that spans decades
