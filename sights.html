Landmarks

Many of Edinburgh’s iconic landmarks appear in phenomenal works of literature. Be sure to take in some of these incredible sights during your literary tour of the city.

The Forth Rail Bridge

The Forth Bridge, an incredible feat of engineering and a UNESCO World Heritage Site, is the setting for Iain Banks remarkable novel The Bridge. It also featured in Alfred Hitchcock’s film adaptation of The 39 Steps, a novel by Scots author John Buchan.

Arthur's Seat

Arthur's Seat, the breathtaking former volcano at the heart of Edinburgh, not only offers stunning views over the city; it has also played key roles as a setting in novels such as The Private Memoirs and Confessions of A Justified Sinner by James Hogg, The Heart of Midlothian by Sir Walter Scott and One Day by David Nicholls.

Edinburgh Castle

Fans of Game of Thrones will no doubt be familiar with the infamous Red Wedding scene. But did you know that George RR Martin’s inspiration came from real-life events at Edinburgh Castle, known as “The Black Dinner”? Learn about this, and many more gruesome tales from the castle’s long past, on one of the many daily tours.

Monuments

Edinburgh’s appreciation for literature can be found in the very structure of the city.

The Burns Monument on Calton Hill

The Burns Monument on Calton Hill crowns the iconic, postcard view of Edinburgh’s skyline. The monument, a small temple in the Neo-Greek design so beloved of Georgian Edinburgh, honours Robert Burns, Scotland’s national bard and one of the leading poets of the Romantic movement.

The Scott Monument in Princes Street Gardens

The Scott Monument pierces the skies above Princes Street and is the second largest construction on Earth built to honour a writer. The striking gothic spire has graced Edinrburgh’s city centre since 1846 and honours Sir Walter Scott, “The Wizard of the North”. Scott, author of world renowned novels such as Ivanhoe, Rob Roy and The Bride of Lammermoor was the most beloved author of his age and has left a lasting legacy on world literature. Visitors can enjoy a stunning panorama of the capital’s skyline by climbing the monument’s spiral staircase.

Other monuments

Exploring Edinburgh will lead you to finding a whole host of other literary monuments.

For example, Makar’s Court, in front of the Writers’ Museum, is comprised of memorial flagstones dedicated to Scots writers; each flagstone is engraved with a quote by a notable author or poet. Fans of Sherlock Holmes will enjoy the statue of the immortal detective in honour of his Edinburgh-born creator, Arthur Conan Doyle. And, on the Royal Mile, visitors can enjoy a plaque decicated to Robert Louis Stevenson in the breathtaking gothic structure of St Giles Cathdral

Tours

What better way for a bookworm to discover Edinburgh than with a literary walking tour of the city?

The Book Break Literary Tour of Edinburgh

If you want to set your own pace, you might like to follow this nifty tour.

Guided Tours

If you’d prefer a sage local guide to bring the literature of the city to life, we have a number of options for you.

The Book Lover’s Tour of Edinburgh provides a fascinating and entertaining overview of Edinburgh’s literary history from past to present. While If you’re the type who gets thirsty while walking, perhaps The Edinburgh Literary Pub Tour is more your speed.

As Harry Potter fans will know, JK Rowling created her boy wizard while living in Edinburgh. And for those looking for a touch more magic is their tour, The Potter Trail takes muggles and wizards alike on the trail of all the sights related to the Harry Potter universe.

Festivals

Not for nothing, Edinburgh is often referred to as ‘Festival City’. Barely a month goes by without some form of cultural festival taking over the city. Here we refer you to three such festivals that take place in the month of August, festivals sure to delight any literature lover.

The Edinburgh International Book Festival

Where else to start but the Edinburgh International Book Festival. Here book lovers from across the globe congregate to discuss literature, meet their favourite authors and get their hands on the latest releases.

The Edinburgh International Festival

The Edinburgh International Festival also draws a range of world-renowned writers to events in Scotland’s capital. Last year, for exampple, national treasure Stephen Fry toured his latest book in Edinburgh

The Edinburgh Fringe Festival

And be sure not to miss the glorious chaos of the Fringe Festival ,where, in amongst the music, and the street performers and the comedy, literature lovers can find plays performed by theatre companies from all over the world taking place in every corner of the city.

Libraries and museums

No literary tour of Edinburgh would be complete without a visit to the capital’s literary museums and Scotland’s national library.

The Writers' Museum

The Writers’ Museum primarily honours Scotland’s literary ‘big three’, Robert Burns, Sir Walter Scott and Robert Louis Stevenson. However, visitors can find books by, information on and events surrounding numerous other Scottish authors.

The Scottish Storytelling Center

The Scottish Storytelling Centre offers visitors the unique experience of taking part in Scotland’s living literary history through live events. Be sure to check out the fine oral storytelling tradition of Scotland while you’re in town.

The National Library of Scotland

And keep an eye out for unique literary events open to the public at The National Library of Scotland.
